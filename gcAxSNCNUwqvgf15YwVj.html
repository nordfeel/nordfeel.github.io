A few myths about hangovers

A few myths about hangovers

Myths about hangovers and how to deal with them are diverse, in particular, much is said about drinks that can lead to this unpleasant state in the early morning. You may be surprised that many of the rules drinking alcoholic beverages that you thought were true is actually completely wrong.

Myth: only a serious overdose of alcohol leads to a morning headache, a couple of glasses can not lead to a hangover.

Truth: even a couple of cups can cause a serious headache for the next day morning, but of course, large doses of alcohol will inevitably lead to a hangover. There is no defined, clearly defined amount of alcohol that can lead to a hangover. Everything is individual and depends on the body. To reduce the effects of a hangover, drink plenty of fluids the next day to replenish your body electrolytes. By the way, the fact that water prevents a hangover, if it is consumed immediately after drinking, is also a myth. Joint intake of alcohol and water or taking water immediately after alcohol does not affect the hangover, since the severe condition in the morning is caused by alcohol intoxication (products of ethanol decomposition and others factors).

Myth: a hangover lasts only one day and does not harm your health.

True: alcohol, especially in large quantities, changes the biochemical balance in the body. In particular, it disrupts the balance of neuroreceptors and leads to severe dehydration of the body. A few hours after a hangover (up to a maximum of you will recover from the harmful physical effects of alcohol, such as headaches and fatigue, but alcohol also has a long-term negative effect on the immune system.

Myth: one aspirin tablet while drinking alcohol can prevent a headache for the next day.

Truth: while drinking alcohol, the use of aspirin or other medications is prohibited. Aspirin increases acid secretion in the stomach and does not contribute to the rapid elimination of alcohol from the body. On the contrary, it was found that aspirin increases the level of alcohol in the blood by up to 26%.

Myth: a snack after drinking protects you from a hangover.

True: eating carbohydrates or high-fat foods before drinking alcohol can protect you from a hangover to some extent, but eating after you've already consumed alcohol will not help at all.

Myth: coffee helps with hangover.

Truth: the caffeine contained in coffee has the ability to dehydrate the body. For this reason, coffee after a good drink can only lead to further dehydration and a worse hangover. It is better to bet on water and decaf drinks.

Myth: wine does not cause hangovers.

True: wine contains tannins, organic compounds that cause severe headaches for some people. In addition to wine, whiskey, which contains malt, also causes severe headaches. In this regard, beer and rectified alcohol (vodka) are safer and do not lead to severe headaches such as wine, whiskey, or tequila.

Myth: alcohol improves sleep.

The truth: alcohol actually disrupts sleep. A single glass of alcohol can actually help you fall asleep more easily, but the constant intake of alcoholic beverages seriously disrupts sleep. People who drink alcohol before going to bed, usually Wake up much earlier than physiologically necessary.
